Community-Acquired Pneumonia Management Guideline (Sample, Public Domain Style)

Diagnosis

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) should be suspected in patients presenting with
cough, fever, dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, and new infiltrate on chest imaging.
Diagnosis is confirmed by the combination of clinical findings and a new pulmonary
opacity on chest radiograph or CT that is not otherwise explained.

Severity Assessment

Severity assessment tools such as CURB-65 or the Pneumonia Severity Index (PSI) should
be used to guide site-of-care decisions. CURB-65 assigns one point each for confusion,
blood urea nitrogen greater than 19 mg/dL, respiratory rate 30 breaths per minute or
greater, systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg or diastolic below 60 mmHg, and age 65 or
older. A CURB-65 score of 0-1 suggests outpatient management is reasonable, a score of 2
suggests consideration of a short inpatient stay or closely supervised outpatient
treatment, and a score of 3 or higher suggests hospitalization, with a score of 4-5
suggesting consideration of ICU-level care.

Outpatient Empiric Therapy

For previously healthy outpatients without comorbidities or recent antibiotic use,
amoxicillin is the preferred empiric agent. Alternatives include doxycycline or, in
areas with low pneumococcal macrolide resistance, a macrolide such as azithromycin. For
outpatients with comorbidities (chronic heart, lung, liver, or renal disease; diabetes;
alcohol use disorder; malignancy; or asplenia), combination therapy with amoxicillin-
clavulanate plus a macrolide or doxycycline, or monotherapy with a respiratory
fluoroquinolone, is recommended.

Inpatient Empiric Therapy

For non-ICU inpatients, recommended regimens include a beta-lactam (such as ampicillin-
sulbactam, cefotaxime, ceftriaxone, or ceftaroline) combined with a macrolide, or
monotherapy with a respiratory fluoroquinolone. For ICU patients, a beta-lactam
combined with either a macrolide or a respiratory fluoroquinolone is recommended. If
risk factors for Pseudomonas aeruginosa are present, an antipseudomonal beta-lactam
should be used. If risk factors for MRSA are present, vancomycin or linezolid should be
added, with de-escalation guided by culture results.

Duration of Therapy

Antibiotic therapy duration for CAP is generally guided by clinical stability rather
than a fixed number of days. A minimum of five days of therapy is recommended, and
therapy should not be discontinued until the patient is afebrile and clinically stable
for 48-72 hours.

Adjunctive Measures

Corticosteroids are not routinely recommended for CAP outside of the setting of
refractory septic shock. Procalcitonin measurement may assist in decisions about
antibiotic initiation and duration but should not override clinical judgment. All
hospitalized patients should be assessed for venous thromboembolism prophylaxis and
early mobilization.
