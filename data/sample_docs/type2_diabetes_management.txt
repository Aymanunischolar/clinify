Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus Management Guideline (Sample, Public Domain Style)

Diagnosis

Type 2 diabetes mellitus is diagnosed by a fasting plasma glucose of 126 mg/dL or
higher, a 2-hour plasma glucose of 200 mg/dL or higher during a 75g oral glucose
tolerance test, a hemoglobin A1c of 6.5% or higher, or a random plasma glucose of 200
mg/dL or higher in a patient with classic symptoms of hyperglycemia. In the absence of
unequivocal hyperglycemia, results should be confirmed with repeat testing.

Glycemic Targets

A reasonable A1c target for most non-pregnant adults is less than 7%. Less stringent
targets (such as less than 8%) may be appropriate for patients with limited life
expectancy, extensive comorbid illness, or a history of severe hypoglycemia. More
stringent targets (such as less than 6.5%) may be considered for selected patients if
achievable without significant hypoglycemia.

First-Line Pharmacotherapy

Metformin remains the preferred initial pharmacologic agent for most patients with type
2 diabetes, given its efficacy, low hypoglycemia risk, favorable cost, and possible
cardiovascular benefit. Metformin should be used with caution or avoided in patients
with an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) below 30 mL/min/1.73m2 due to risk
of lactic acidosis.

Second-Line Therapy and Cardiovascular/Renal Risk

In patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or
chronic kidney disease, a GLP-1 receptor agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor with demonstrated
cardiovascular or renal benefit should be added independent of A1c control, given
evidence of reduced major adverse cardiovascular events, heart failure hospitalization,
and progression of kidney disease. SGLT2 inhibitors carry a risk of euglycemic diabetic
ketoacidosis and genital mycotic infections and should be used cautiously in patients at
risk of volume depletion.

Insulin Therapy

Insulin should be considered at any stage of type 2 diabetes when glycemic targets are
not met, particularly in the setting of significant hyperglycemia (A1c above 10% or
blood glucose persistently above 300 mg/dL), catabolic features, or acute illness.
Basal insulin is typically the preferred starting regimen, titrated based on fasting
glucose readings.

Monitoring and Complication Screening

A1c should be checked at least twice yearly in patients meeting treatment goals and
quarterly in patients not at goal or with recent therapy changes. Annual screening
should include a dilated eye exam, foot examination with monofilament testing, urine
albumin-to-creatinine ratio, and lipid panel. Blood pressure should be checked at every
visit, with a target generally below 130/80 mmHg in most patients with diabetes.

Hypoglycemia Management

Mild to moderate hypoglycemia (blood glucose below 70 mg/dL) in a conscious patient
should be treated with 15-20 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate, followed by rechecking
glucose in 15 minutes. Severe hypoglycemia with altered consciousness should be treated
with intramuscular or intranasal glucagon, or intravenous dextrose if in a monitored
setting.
