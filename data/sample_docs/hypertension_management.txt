Hypertension Management Guideline (Sample, Public Domain Style)

Overview

Hypertension is defined as a sustained systolic blood pressure of 130 mmHg or higher,
or a diastolic blood pressure of 80 mmHg or higher, confirmed on at least two separate
occasions. It is a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and
chronic kidney disease.

Classification

Normal blood pressure is defined as systolic below 120 mmHg and diastolic below 80 mmHg.
Elevated blood pressure is systolic 120-129 mmHg with diastolic below 80 mmHg.
Stage 1 hypertension is systolic 130-139 mmHg or diastolic 80-89 mmHg.
Stage 2 hypertension is systolic 140 mmHg or higher, or diastolic 90 mmHg or higher.
A hypertensive crisis is systolic above 180 mmHg and/or diastolic above 120 mmHg and
requires immediate clinical evaluation.

Initial Evaluation

Initial evaluation should include a thorough history, physical examination, basic
metabolic panel, lipid panel, urinalysis, and a 12-lead ECG. Clinicians should screen
for secondary causes of hypertension in patients presenting at a young age, with
resistant hypertension, or with an abrupt onset of severe hypertension.

Lifestyle Interventions

All patients with elevated blood pressure or hypertension should be counseled on
lifestyle modification, including the DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
eating pattern, sodium restriction to less than 2,300 mg per day (ideally 1,500 mg for
most adults with hypertension), regular aerobic exercise of at least 150 minutes per
week, weight loss for patients with overweight or obesity, limiting alcohol intake, and
smoking cessation.

Pharmacologic Therapy

For patients with stage 1 hypertension and an estimated 10-year atherosclerotic
cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk of 10% or greater, or with stage 2 hypertension,
pharmacologic therapy should be initiated in addition to lifestyle changes. First-line
agents include thiazide-type diuretics, calcium channel blockers (CCBs), and
angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACE inhibitors) or angiotensin receptor
blockers (ARBs). ACE inhibitors and ARBs should not be combined due to increased risk of
hyperkalemia and renal impairment. Beta-blockers are not considered first-line for
uncomplicated hypertension unless there is a compelling indication such as coronary
artery disease or heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.

Follow-Up and Monitoring

Blood pressure should be reassessed within one month of initiating or adjusting therapy.
Once blood pressure is at goal and stable, follow-up every three to six months is
reasonable. Home blood pressure monitoring is recommended to confirm office
measurements and to monitor treatment response.

Special Populations

In patients with diabetes mellitus or chronic kidney disease with albuminuria, an ACE
inhibitor or ARB is preferred as first-line therapy due to renoprotective effects. In
pregnancy, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and direct renin inhibitors are contraindicated due to
teratogenicity; labetalol, nifedipine, and methyldopa are preferred agents.
